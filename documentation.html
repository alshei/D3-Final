dataset

The dataset for this project can be found here. It includes six attributes: lung capacity, age, height, smoke, gender, and caesarean. More specifically, these refer to the closing capacity of an individual, their age, their height, whether or not the individual smokes, their gender, and whether or not the individual was born from a caesarean. The age group of the individuals range between 3 and 19, and their heights range between 45 to 85 inches.

design process

As I learned how to use D3.js from the book, Interactive Data Visualization for the Web by Scott Murray, I created practice visualizations that are contained within the practice folder of this project. They consisted primarily of bar charts and scatterplots and I gained an understanding of how to use multiple datasets, dynamics datasets, animations, tooltips, and more.

Following this practice, my design process for the lung capacity dataset started with putting the data into Tableau and comparing different attributes to one another. Age and height both appeared to have strong correlation with lung closing capacity as my mockup charts show. I filtered the data to the individuals that did not smoke because there were more data points to observe. I also realised that I preferred to have height and age on the x-axis and independent variables to the y-axis, lung capacity.

rationale of design choices

After deciding that these were the attributes I wished to explore in my visualization, I began to parse the data in D3.js and construct my charts. A scatterplot seemed like the most suitable chart type for this dataset since each individual point contains information about an individual's height/age and lung closing capacity.

The axes were originally both from 0 to the upper level of lung capacity and height/age, but this left too much empty space on the left and bottom parts of the chart. For this reason, I trimmed the range of both the x-axis and y-axis to dynamically include the min and max values of each attribute.

While my points were initally solid dots, I ended up leaning towards my mockup charts and creating rings out of them; I felt that doing this allowed the viewer to better observe overlapping data points as they did not completely obscure one another.

I wished to explore multiple attributes in the same chart and so I created buttons to toggle between height and age so that the correlation between both attributes and lung closing capacity could be observed. Animations were used to make the transitions smooth and appealing to look at, and I included a subtle gradient to the data where a more reddish color is used for the lower end of the cc values and a more purplish color is used for the higher end of the cc values.

Finally, I added tooltips to each of the data points so that the viewer might be able to mouseover a point and see its exact values. I think this is important to have as an option, even though visualizations are made to make data easily digestible at first glance.

conclusions

Based on the information in the dataset and the way it is portrayed in my visualzation, there appears to be a strong correlation between height and lung closing capacity as well as age and lung closing capacity. The taller or older an individual is, it is more likely that they have a greater closing capacity than an individual that is shorter or younger than them. In other words, lung closing capacity appears to increase with height and age.

I find myself curious to learn more about how lung capacity increases over the course of a person's life and wonder how much difference is found after the childhood and adolescent stage of a person's life. Because a 19 year old is nearly an adult and most people stop growing in height after their teenage years, I wonder if there continues to be a linear growth in lung capacity due to age, or if there is a point where the data begins to flatten out due to height.
